The Problem

Company knowledge is everywhere and nowhere.

Every organization accumulates a mountain of institutional knowledge — decisions made in Zoom calls, context shared in Slack threads, documents living in Google Drive folders nobody remembers. This knowledge is siloed, unstructured, and practically invisible to AI agents trying to answer questions or take actions on behalf of employees.

The goal of this project: build a system where an AI agent can ask a question that spans multiple data sources and get a coherent, contextually grounded answer — without a human having to manually connect the dots.

Architecture

Graph-native, agent-first.

The system is built around a Neo4j knowledge graph as the central store — not a vector database, not a SQL table. Relationships between entities (people, decisions, documents, meetings) are first-class citizens. This allows agents to traverse connections that a flat retrieval system would miss entirely.

Data Sources

Three sources. One graph.

📹
Zoom Transcripts
Meeting recordings are transcribed and parsed into entities: who said what, what decisions were made, what action items were assigned. These become nodes and relationships in the graph.
💬
Slack Threads
Conversations are ingested and structured — linking topics, people, channels, and time. Agents can trace how a decision evolved through async discussion, not just find the final message.
📁
Google Drive Documents
Reports, specs, and presentations are parsed for entities and linked to the people and conversations that reference them — creating a connected document graph, not a flat search index.
Process

Discovery-driven architecture.

Graph architecture decisions were grounded in end-user discovery sessions — understanding what questions employees actually need to answer, how they currently navigate company knowledge, and where existing tools fail them.

Live user feedback was also captured as modeling data: when an agent gave a wrong or incomplete answer, that failure signal was used to refine the graph schema and retrieval strategies iteratively. The architecture evolved alongside real usage.
